The problem

Trybe had a solid family management app — shared calendars, routines, reminders — and a small but loyal user base. The question was: how do you scale without burning budget on ads that families ignore?

"Families don't download new utilities for fun. We needed growth that reached parents where they already trust someone else."

Our brief: find growth that's trustworthy, repeatable, and low-cost. I led partnership strategy and UX insight — translating research into a plan that tied real partners to measurable outcomes.

The approach
1
Scan the ecosystem
Mapped the UK parenting landscape — schools, nurseries, NHS, councils, employers, influencer collectives. Benchmarked family apps for activation loops and retention levers.
2
Score partnerships
Built a heat-map matrix scoring each partner type on audience reach, trust level, onboarding ease, activation potential, and cost. Schools and nurseries emerged as the sweet spot.
3
Design three lanes
Each lane had its own messaging, materials, and onboarding flow. The guiding principle: borrow trust, then simplify.
What we built

Lane A — Schools & PTAs. Co-branded onboarding kits: QR posters, newsletter snippets, a teacher quick-start guide, and a privacy promise card. Copy written to sound like a teacher, not a marketer.

Lane B — Employers & HR. Positioned Trybe as a wellbeing tool. Less calendar chaos at home means better focus at work. Delivered HR email templates, co-branded landing pages, and a back-to-school campaign calendar.

Lane C — Nurseries. Front-desk QR stands and handout cards. Onboarding simple enough to explain in 15 seconds between pickups.

Outcomes

Four weeks of secondary research produced a ready-to-act growth framework — not ads, not guesswork. The team left with a 30-page strategy deck, a full artefact set, and a measurement framework defining four behavioural indicators:

Family groups created within 24h Both parents onboarded within 72h First shared action within 3 days Week-4 retained families
What I learned

Growth design isn't about louder messaging — it's about designing trust. This project sharpened my ability to connect strategic logic with human behaviour, proving that even business frameworks can have empathy baked in.

The question

Can a no-code VR tool deliver structured procedural training that's actually comparable to hands-on practice? And where does each modality perform better?

"The goal wasn't to prove VR is better. It was to understand where it helps, where it hinders, and how to design it accordingly."

I built a chemical spill response training simulation in ShapesXR and a physical real-world twin of the same scenario — then ran a controlled comparison.

Design decisions
1
4 cognitive chunks, not 7 stages
Early walkthroughs showed users weren't failing the task — they were failing cognitive juggling. Simplified 7 stages into 4 chunks: Identify hazard → Select PPE → Contain spill → Dispose & report. One scene = one mental state.
2
Narration as the instruction layer
ShapesXR has no branching logic or interactive feedback. So narration did that work — 10–12 second voice instructions, timed to scene transitions, so guidance arrived only when visually relevant.
3
Accessibility first, not last
No gesture controls, no radial menus, no hidden interactions. Single input: point + click. Information always visible — never recalled from memory. Designed for zero prior VR experience.
4
Low fidelity by design
Abstract shapes, flat colours. Research shows low-fidelity visuals shift focus to process, not appearance — and reduce motion sickness for VR-naive users.
What the study found

VR users took 1.18 minutes longer — not from confusion, but from deliberate environmental scanning before acting. Slower, more intentional decision-making.

Fewer procedural errors in VR, particularly at hazard identification and PPE selection. Participants paused, looked around, cross-verified with the manual. Physical participants relied on muscle memory — faster execution, more skipped steps.

Manual checking was nearly identical across both conditions — validating the choice to embed a persistent instruction panel in every scene.

"VR excels at deliberate decision-making. Physical rehearsal excels at fluent execution. They are not competing — they are cognitively complementary."

The brief

Build a fun, child-friendly 2D platformer for ages 10–14. Dodge meteors, collect gems, survive as long as you can. Ship a polished vertical slice in four weeks with a cross-disciplinary team of six.

I wore two hats: UX lead (game feel, interface, player feedback) and Scrum producer (sprint planning, backlog, retros, keeping a mixed HCI/CS team aligned).

My UX focus
1
Controls for 10-year-olds
Arrow keys to move, shift to dash. No tutorial walls. An onboarding screen that teaches through play, not text. Designed so a new player is competent within 30 seconds.
2
Difficulty curve, not difficulty cliff
Tuned meteor speed and spawn frequency through playtesting. The game had to be challenging enough to keep 10–14 year olds engaged, but not so hard it caused rage-quits in the first minute.
3
Feedback that feels good
Screen shake on meteor hit. Visual and audio cues for incoming danger. Every action had an immediate, satisfying response — critical for keeping young players oriented and motivated.
Producing the sprint

Running a team of six across two disciplines meant making sure HCI and CS students were solving the same problem — not parallel ones. I ran weekly sprint planning, managed the Trello backlog, led retros, and handled peer-review gates before any code hit the dev branch.

The process worked. We shipped everything planned, with a week of buffer for polish. Retrospectives flagged one thing to do better: estimate team velocity more tightly around reading week.

What shipped
Polished vertical slice Time-based leaderboard Tuned difficulty curve 3-background settings Onboarding tutorial Shipped on schedule
